Lecture 2 - Elastic scattering of WIMPs#

Because we don’t know exactly what a WIMP is, we have to make some assumptions about properties we would expect all WIMPs to have in order to detect them. The most robust direct searches for WIMPs, which it turns out also have the greatest sensitivities, make the assumption that all WIMPs, no matter what their theoretical origin, will scatter elastically off a nucleus. By elastic scattering, we mean that before and after the scatter, the species involved are the same. So, in goes a WIMP and strikes a nucleus, and after the collision you are left with the same WIMP and the same species of nucleus. Have a think about why this corresponds to the meaning of elastic scattering in ordinary classical physics. The nucleus, however, has gained some energy, and it turns out that this energy could be sufficient, with the right choice of detector material, to give rise to signals that could potentially be detected in a variety of different ways.

An additional important assumption made by all direct searches is that WIMPs are all around us in the laboratory. This is true if the dark matter halo of our galaxy exists, consists of WIMPs, and is sufficiently smooth so that there is a non-zero density everywhere within the halo. Recall from Vik’s lectures on the local dark matter halo that the energy density of dark matter in the vicinity of Earth is thought to be something like \(\rm \rho_H=0.3\,GeV\,cm^{-3}\). This is the total energy density of the WIMP dark matter, dominated by the energy associated the rest masses of the WIMPs.

Since WIMP masses, though not known with any precision, have their mass scale set by the masses of the known weak vector bosons, we might start by making detectors having particles to scatter with WIMPs that have masses of order \(\rm 100\,GeV/c^2\), similar to the masses of the \(\rm W\) and \(\rm Z\) bosons. Again, very fortunately, ordinary matter contains many targets of about this mass, the nuclei in atoms. If we choose a target material that has nuclei with masses, in atomic mass units, of around 100, these masses are of the same order as the scale for WIMP masses. Again, we don’t know the WIMP mass for sure to even within an order of magnitude, but in the absence of better information this mass scale is a good place to start. Common targets are Xenon (atomic mass 131AMU), Iodine (127), Germanium (73) and Tungsten (184). These elements may be parts of molecules in a substance that has some observerable behaviour when the nucleus recoils. More about that in Lecture 4.

In addition, nuclei in atoms are essentially stationary with respect to the lab frame. So we have a compelling and simple picture of a WIMP of mass of order \(\rm 100\,GeV\) coming in with a velocity typical of that of particles in our local dark matter halo, so of order \(\rm 200\,km/s\), and striking a nucleus at rest. The nucleus gains some energy and recoils. The WIMP survives the collision and emerges but does not interact further in the detector. Because it is an elastic collision, both energy and momentum are conserved.

Overall WIMP elastic scattering rate for an idealised detector#

Let us consider an idealised detector consisting of a cuboid of nuclei of some element, atomic number \(A\). The cuboid is of height \(H\), width \(W\) and length \(L\), and contains a mass \(M\) in kilograms of the detecting element. The number of nuclei available for WIMPs to scatter off, \(N\), is

\[\begin{align} N&=\frac{1000MN_A}{A}, \tag{2.1} \end{align}\]

where \(N_A\) is Avogadro’s number and the factor of \(\rm 1000\) occurs because \(A\) is the number of grams in a mole of a substance. Now, let us consider WIMPs having some velocity \(v\) incident normal to the end of this detector, height \(H\) and width \(W\). From the perspective of the incoming WIMP, there are \(N\) target particles. The probability of elastic scattering off any one of the nuclei is encoded in the elastic scattering cross section for the nucleus. This is the classical area that the incoming WIMP has to strike in order for any elastic scatter to occur. This area is the same for each nucleus, as they are all physically identical. These cross sections are such tiny areas that the probability of the WIMP scattering twice is negligible. Let us use \(\sigma_{\rm NUC}\) to denote this elastic scattering cross section off a nuclus.

Since each nucleus is independent of the others, the total cross section for elastic scattering off any one of the nuclei is the number of nuclei times \(\sigma_{\rm NUC}\). So, the total cross section for scattering in the detector, \(\sigma_{\rm DET}\), is

\[\begin{align} \sigma_{\rm DET}&=N\sigma_{\rm NUC}=\frac{1000MN_A\sigma_{\rm NUC}}{A}. \tag{2.2} \end{align}\]

Since the nuclei are distributed roughly uniformly throughout the volume, the probability of our WIMP intersecting one of the cross sections required for scattering is given by the ratio of this cross section to the total area of the detector to the incoming WIMP, which is \(WH\). Therefore the probability \(p_{\rm 1e}\) of a single WIMP undergoing an elastic scatter in the detector is

\[\begin{align} p_{\rm 1e}&=\frac{\sigma_{\rm DET}}{WH}=\frac{1000MN_A\sigma_{\rm NUC}}{AWH}. \tag{2.3} \end{align}\]

Now consider a population of WIMPs all having this same velocity \(v\). Suppose that the number density of these WIMPs is \(n\) per cubic metre. If they are all approaching the detector in the same direction, then the flux, the number of WIMPs crossing a unit area of detector surface into the detector per unit time, is given by \(\Phi=nv\). This is because the common velocity of the WIMPs is \(v\) metres per second, so \(v\) metres cubed passes into the detector in a second for each \(\rm m^2\) of the detector surface. However, the area of the end of the detector is \(WH\) so that the number of WIMPs entering the detector per second is \(\Phi WH=nvWH\). Each of these WIMPs has a probability of \(p_{\rm 1e}\) of scattering. Hence the rate at which elastic scatters occurs between this population of WIMPs and the nuclei in the detector, \(R_{\rm TOT}(v)\), is

\[\begin{align} R_{\rm TOT}(v)&=\frac{1000MN_A\sigma_{\rm NUC}nv}{A}. \tag{2.4} \end{align}\]

Notice that this scattering rate depends on the total mass \(M\) of the detector, but not the ratio of its side lengths. Any cuboid will have the same scattering rate. In fact, it turns out that only the amount of detector material, the number of nuclei, is present in the rate formula. You can make a WIMP detector any shape you like, and optimise its shape to suit the detectors that are instrumenting it.

WIMP nucleon elastic scattering modelled as a collision between hard spheres#

The easiest and most common way of analysing the scattering of nuclei by WIMPs is to treat the collision as if both the WIMP and the target nucleus are hard spheres. In fact, this model is only partly satisfactory. Though it gives some useful insights, some of the results from it are inaccurate, in places highly so. Nonetheless, we shall start by considering sphere scattering and see what it leads us to conclude. The hard sphere scattering model is illustrated below.

Notice that the angle at which the nucleus is scattered is determined classically by whether the collision is a glancing blow or more head-on. The measure of this concept classically is a distance called the impact parameter, labelled with a \(b\) in the diagram. If the impact parameter is less than the sum of the radii of the spheres representing WIMP and nucleus, then the collision occurs. Otherwise, the WIMP misses the nucleus and there is no recoil. This very classical framework is in line with the cross section framework described above, with the cross section for the collision given by \(\pi R^2\), \(R\) being the sum of the classical radii of the WIMP and the nucleus. However, we might wonder whether this totally classical picture of what happens is really appropriate. This is all material to be discussed in Lecture 3.

We will use \(\theta\) to denote the angle that the nuclear recoil direction makes with the incident direction of the WIMP in the lab. Conservation of momentum means that the momentum vectors of the incident and scattered WIMP together with the momentum of the scattered nucleus make a non-right-angled triangle. Conservation of momentum means that

\[\begin{align} \vec{p_f}&=\vec{p_w}-\vec{p_r}, \tag{2.5} \end{align}\]

where \(\vec{p_w}\) is the momentum for the incoming WIMP, \(\vec{p_f}\) is the momentum of the outgoing wimp, and \(\vec{p_r}\) is the momentum of the recoiling nucleus after the collision. Taking the dot product of this equation with itself, we get

\[\begin{split}\begin{align} \vec{p_f}\cdot\vec{p_f}&=(\vec{p_w}-\vec{p_r})\cdot(\vec{p_w}-\vec{p_r}) \nonumber \\ p_f^2 &= p_w^2 + p_r^2 -2\vec{p_w}\cdot\vec{p_r} \nonumber \\ &= p_w^2 + p_r^2 -2p_wp_r\cos\theta, \tag{2.6} \end{align}\end{split}\]

where \(p_w\), \(p_f\) and \(p_r\) are the magnitudes of the momentua of the incoming WIMP, outgoing WIMP, and recoiling nucleus, respectively, so \(p_w=\sqrt{\vec{p_w}\cdot\vec{p_w}}\), and so on. You might notice that the above algebra is effectively a derivation of the cosine rule for the sides and angles of non right angled triangles, and indeed we could have just written down the last line by quoting this result.

Along with momentum, total energy is conserved. In elastic scattering, because the particle species remain the same throughout, and there is no potential energy giving rise to a force field, conservation of total energy leads to conservation of total kinetic energy. Because the incoming WIMP has velocity of order \(0.001c\), the process is non-relativistic, so we may write the kinetic energy of a particle of mass \(m\) as \((1/2)mv^2=p^2/(2m)\). So we can express conservation of energy as

\[\begin{align} \frac{p_w^2}{2m_w}&=\frac{p_f^2}{2m_w}+\frac{p_r^2}{2m_N} \tag{2.7} \end{align}\]

Multiplying by \(2m_w\) throughout and rearranging, we obtain

\[\begin{align} p_f^2&=p_w^2-\frac{m_w}{m_N}p_r^2. \tag{2.8} \end{align}\]

When we substitute this result into Equation 2.6, we can eliminate \(p_f\)< and some further cancellation occurs.

\[\begin{split}\begin{align} p_w^2-\frac{m_w}{m_N}p_r^2&=p_w^2 + p_r^2 -2p_wp_r\cos\theta \nonumber \\ 2p_wp_r\cos\theta &= p_r^2\left(1+\frac{m_w}{m_N}\right) \nonumber \\ p_r &= \frac{2p_w\cos\theta}{\left(1+\frac{m_w}{m_N}\right)}. \tag{2.9} \end{align}\end{split}\]

The more experimentally interesting quantity is the recoil kinetic energy \(E_R\) of the WIMP in the lab, which is given by

\[\begin{split}\begin{align} E_R=\frac{p_r^2}{2m_N} &= \frac{4p_w^2\cos^2\theta}{2m_N\left(1+\frac{m_w}{m_N}\right)^2} \nonumber \\ &=\frac{2m_w^2m_Nv_w^2\cos^2\theta}{(m_N+m_w)^2} \nonumber \\ &=\frac{2\mu^2v_w^2}{m_N}\cos^2\theta, \tag{2.10} \end{align}\end{split}\]

where the quantity \(\mu\) is called the reduced mass of the WIMP and nucleus, defined by

\[\begin{align} \mu=\frac{m_wm_N}{m_w+m_N}. \tag{2.11} \end{align}\]

Looking again at the figure, we note using the small right angled triangle to the right of the centre of mass of the WIMP at the point of scattering that \(\sin\theta=b/R\), so that \(\cos\theta=\sqrt{1-b^2/R^2}\). We substitute this into the expression for the recoil energy and obtain

\[\begin{align} E_R&=\frac{2\mu^2v_w^2}{m_N}\left(1-\frac{b^2}{R^2}\right). \tag{2.12} \end{align}\]

As discussed before, the maximum recoil is expected when the collision is head-on, in other words, when \(b=0\). The maximum recoil energy is therefore \(E_R^{\rm max}=2\mu^2 v_w^2/m_N\). It is useful to calculate this for an example. Inserting factors of \(c\) so that all masses are re-expressed as energies at rest, we get

\[\begin{align} E_R^{\rm max}=\frac{2\left({\mu c^2}\right)^2\left(\frac{v_w}{c}\right)^2}{m_Nc^2}. \tag{2.13} \end{align}\]

If the WIMP velocity is \(\rm 240\,km/s\), then \(v_w/c={\rm 8\times10^{-4}}\). If the WIMP mass and the Nuclear mass are both \(\rm 100\,GeV/c^2\) then \(\mu c^2={\rm 50\,GeV}\), and \(m_Nc^2={\rm 100\,GeV}\). Substituting all these in we get \(E_R^{\rm max}={\rm 3.2\times10^{-5}\,GeV}\), or \(\rm 32\, keV\). This is essentially a best case scenario, where the WIMP and nuclear masses are identical and there is a head-on collision. Note also that at higher values of the recoil energy, quantum effects modify the recoil energy that you get in practice, as discussed in Lecture 3.

Whether recoil energies like this are detectable depends on whether this amount of energy is enough to disrupt existing bound systems in the vicinity of the recoiling nucleus. It is encouraging to note that typical atomic binding energies are of order \(\rm 10\,eV\), (the binding energy of the electron in hydrogen in its ground state is \(\rm 13.6\,eV\)). We might therefore expect to be able to derive an observable signature from the debris resulting from the disruption of the orbits of atomic electrons as a consequence of the recoiling nucleus. The nuclear recoil energy is sufficient to ionise or excite multiple neighbouring atoms, or perhaps to cause other processes such as the emission of phonons, quanta of lattice vibration, or cause local material to undergo phase transitions. These possible observational signatures will be discussed further in Lecture 4.

Probablity distribution for recoil energies in the hard sphere model#

This maximum recoil energy isn’t interesting if it turns out that almost all collisions are glancing blows, with much smaller values of the recoil energy. In the hard sphere model, you can use simple geometry to work out the relative probablities of different values of the impact factor \(b\). From the perspective of the incoming WIMP, the target nucleus presents itself as a circle of radius \(R\). The points having an impact factor in the range \(b\) to \(b+db\) are in an annulus of width \(b\) and circumference \(2\pi b\). The probability of attaining such an impact factor is proportional to the area of this annulus, which is \(2\pi b\,db\). This probability is also a small increment, \(dP\). We therefore write

\[\begin{align} dP=2C\pi b\,db, \tag{2.14} \end{align}\]

where \(C\) is a normalisation constant. We take it as given that there is a collision with this nucleus, and we are only interested in the relative probabilities of different values of \(b\). Then the integral of \(dP\) over all values of \(b\) between \(0\) and \(R\) must be 1, so

\[\begin{split}\begin{align} \int_{b=0}^R dP = 1 &= 2\pi C\int_{b=0}^R b\,db = \pi C R^2. \nonumber \\ C&=\frac{1}{\pi R^2}. \nonumber \\ \frac{dP}{db}=p(b)&=\frac{2b}{R^2}. \tag{2.15} \end{align}\end{split}\]

The quantity \(p(b)\) is called the probability density at impact factor \(b\). You multiply this density by a small interval \(db\) in impact factor to get a probablity for an impact factor in that range. Exactly the same construction applies in the case of cross sections. Howeer, what we are really interested in is the probability density in the recoil energy, \(p(E_R)\), which is the probability density for the recoil energy. Here we interpret \(p(E_R)dE_R\) as the probability for the recoil energy being in the range \(E_R\) to \(E_R+dE_R\). Fortunately, you can use a slightly modified version of the chain rule to convert the probability density in the impact factor to the related one in the recoil energy,

\[\begin{align} p(b)=\frac{dP}{db}=\frac{dP}{dE_R}\left|\frac{dE_R}{db}\right|. \tag{2.16} \end{align}\]

The modulus sign reflects the fact that all the derivatives that represent probability densities are by definition positive. It doesn’t matter that as you decrease \(b\) you increase the recoil energy \(E_R\), adding to the range of \(b\) considered increases the total probability of the impact factor being in that range; adding to the range of \(E_R\) by the corresponding amount also increases the total probability of the recoil energy being in the correspondingly increased range. The sign of the derivative that does the conversion is discarded in line with our convention for the definition of a probablity density. Substituting in for the derivatives from Equations 2.12 and 2.15, we arrive at

\[\begin{split}\begin{align} \frac{2b}{R^2}&=p(E_R)\frac{4\mu^2v_w^2\,b}{m_N\,R^2} \nonumber \\ p(E_R)&=\frac{m_N}{2\mu^2 v_w^2}. \tag{2.17} \end{align}\end{split}\]

This surprisingly simple result implies that the probability density for the recoil energy of a nucleus that has been struck by a WIMP is uniform between zero and the maximum possible recoil energy for a head on collision. Notice that, as expected, the area under the probability density graph is 1. The recoil energy has to be something!

Though this is the prediction of the hard sphere scattering model, it turns out that in practice there are very significant corrections to this result that arise from a more sophisticated quantum treatment of WIMP nuclear scattering that will be discussed in Lecture 3.

WIMP nucleon elastic cross sections#

The underlying process is the interaction of a WIMP with a neutron or a proton. We have reasonable confidence that WIMPs will scatter off neutrons and protons because they are by definition weakly interacting, and the weak gauge bosons, \(\rm W^{\pm}\) and \(\rm Z^0\) have couplings to nucleons. Of course, the nucleons are in turn composed of quarks and gluons, but at the low energies of WIMP elastic scattering it is protons and neutrons, or nucleons, that act as the fundamental particles inside the nucleus. It is at higher energies accessible only in accelerators where you start to see scattering off individual quarks.

Since the fundamental process is between a WIMP and a nucleon, its probability is usually expressed in terms of the WIMP nucleon cross section, which we denote by \(\sigma_N\). This cross section is the same for neutrons and protons because WIMPs are electrically neutral and hence do not couple to the different charges of protons and neutrons. The question is, how is this cross section related to the cross section for the process that actually occurs, which is the elastic scattering of the WIMP off the whole nucleus, \(\sigma_{\rm NUC}\)?

Naively, a nucleus consists of \(A\) nucleons, so you might expect that \(\sigma_{\rm NUC}\) is perhaps equal to \(A\sigma_N\). This assumption is based on treating the nucleons in the nucleus as if they act independently of each other. For the nucleons in a nucleus t act independently in this way, two things would have to be true:

  • The WIMP would have to couple to one nucleon in the nucleus, without having any interaction or coupling to the other nucleons.

  • This single nucleon would then have to scatter out of the nucleus, taking the recoil energy and momentum from the collision and leaving the other nucleons behind.

Neither of these requirements are satisfied. In fact, both in the interaction and in the final state recoil, the nucleons in the nucleus behave coherently. It turns out that this coherence between nucleons enhances the cross section for scattering of a nucleus by WIMPs substantially compared to the naive \(\sigma_{\rm NUC}=A\sigma_N\) that results from assuming incoherent scattering.
