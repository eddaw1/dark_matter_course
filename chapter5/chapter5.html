Detectable Signals from Nuclear Recoils#

Light, and charge, and heat#

The initial result of an elastic collision of a WIMP with a nucleus in a detector is a nucleus that has several \(\rm keV\) of kinetic energy. This nucleus is moving through a detector containing other atoms. The binding energies of atomic electrons to their nuclei are a few \(\rm eV\). Therefore, the kinetic energy of the recoiling nucleus is sufficient to cause many atoms to be excited. These excitations create signals of many kinds, and today we will talk only about the three most common.

Light and scintillation#

Where the recoiling nucleus strikes an atom, it can cause one or more electrons in the struck atom to move from their ground state energy level to an excited state. The excited state electron then de-excites back into its ground state, and the energy lost results in the emission of a photon. This process is known as scintillation. Because the recoil energy of the nucleus is enough to cause multiple atoms to move to excited states, when these excited atoms de-excite, many photons may be emitted at once, resulting in a detectable light pulse. Some solids and liquids are known to be efficient scintillators. If the target, whether it is liquid or solid, is also transparent to the wavelength of light that is produced, then a sensitive light detector can be used to register the pulse. Either the height of the light pulse, or its area, can be used as a measure of the energy released in the pulse, and hence used to estimate the energy of the nuclear recoil.

Charge and ionisation#

The recoil energy is also enough to ionise many atoms, so that their electrons are freed from the bound states available to them entirely. These free electrons may recombine with the atoms that yielded them, or fall into another atom also ionised in the event, in which case again you see a scintillation light signal. However, if the detection medium is in a strong electric field, this field can draw the electron away from the ions and the charged electron can be transported through the detector, before being detected in some way with a sensor at the detector surface. The amount of ionisation charge in an event is again a measure of the recoil energy of the nucleus that caused the ionisation signal. Whether charge can be detected depends on whether free charges can move through the detector. Charge signals can be extracted from some solid and some liquid detectors.

Heat#

If the detector consists of a material having a crystalline structure, then the energy from a nuclear recoil can be converted into vibrationn energy in the crystal. The small amounts of energy involved mean that the excited heat entitites are phonons, which you may remember from solid state physics as the quanta of lattice vibrations. Phonons produce a very small amount of heating as they are collective excitations of the entire detector target, but the right sensing transducer is nonetheless capable of measuring the presence of phonons from the heating resulting from an elastic scattering event.

Dual channel detection.#

It is always better to design a detector so that it is sensitive to two different detectable signals, usually two of the ones described above. The reason is that the ratio of the energy deposited via these two different mechanisms is a powerful way of separating the nuclear recoils that are the signatures of WIMP scattering from the electron recoils produced by background non-WIMP events. More about backgrounds and background rejection later in the lecture.

There are essentially three different two-channel detector types. The first of these uses liquified noble gas, which both emits scintillation light, and allows for liberated charge to drift through the liquid. The second is crystals of semiconductor, germanium or silicon. These also allow charge transport. They are optically opaque so no scintillation can be detected, but when cooled to very low temperatures they can be instrumented with very sensitive thermometers that allow the detection of heating phonons. Finally, there are crystals made of calcium tungstate, which allow the detection of heating phonons and also scintillation light, but are electrically insulating so that electric charge cannot translate to the surface.

Of the three detection technologies, the one that has succeeded in moving to the front, in terms of sensitivity, is noble liquid detector targets with the detection of scintillation light and charge. The main reason for this is that the noble liquid detectors are scalable to very large target masses, leading to higher event rates from a given WIMP population, as well as better self shielding, meaning that the target material itself acts effectively as a shield against background events reaching the heart of the detector.

LUX-ZEPLIN - the most sensitive WIMP detector today.#

Below is a diagram of the LUX-ZEPLIN experiment showing the dual signal readout of a large liquid xenon dark matter target via primary scintillation and the detection of ionisation electrons. The detector is about the height of a grown person.

LUX-ZEPLIN is the worlds leading WIMP detector. It has to date placed limits on WIMP dark matter with WIMP nucleon cross sections as low as \(\rm 2\times10^{-12}\,pb\). The core target consists of liquid xenon, a noble group 8 element that liqifies at \(\rm -110^\circ C\). This very cold tub of liquid xenon is over \(\rm 1000\,kg\) in mass.

Scintillation in LUX-ZEPLIN#

The transparent xenon scintillates when a nucleus in the detector recoils, hopefully as a result of a collision with a dark matter WIMP. This scintillation light is emitted in all directions, and this scintillation light is detected in arrays of light sensors called photomultiplied tubes mounted on the top and bottom of the detector. The light emmitted in xenon scintillation is at a wavelength of around \(\rm 178\,nm\), a vacuum ultra-violet wavelength. Therefore the scintillation light detectors have to be selected to have sensitivity at this wavelength. The light is detected with an array of photomultiplier tubes, a vacuum tube based light detector whose operation will be discussed later in the lecture. The choice of photomultiplier tubes is made because they have large collection area, reasonably high quantum efficiency, and manageable levels of noise. The signal resulting from the primary scintillation light generated close to the point where the original nuclear recoil occurred is referred to pretty universally as the S1 signal.

Ionisation in LUX-ZEPLIN#

In addition, scintillation in xenon ionises some of the xenon atoms. The liberated negative electrons find themselves in an electric field that points downwards. The electric field is a result of a high voltage between a grid of fine wires mounted above the top of the xenon liquid, but below the photomultiplier tubes at the top, and another grid of fine wires mounted in the liquid above the bottom photomultiplier tubes. The field is kept uniform because surrounding the xenon cylinder are finely spaced metallic rings called field shaping rings, held at voltages becoming more positive linearly from the cathode electrode at the top to the anode electrode at the bottom. The electrons, being negative, start to accelerate towards the cathode, but as they are in a liquid they reach a terminal velocity that depends on the voltage, but is of order \(\rm 1000\,m\,s^{-1}\). They continue at this terminal velocity until they reach the surface of the liquid. The detector is designed so that the surface of the liquid xenon is threaded by a larger electric field, in which the electrons break free of the liquid and move into the xenon gas above the lake. Once in the gas, the electrons accelerate rapidly, and this acceleration causes them to emit further light through the electroluminescence effect, resulting in a second signal in the photomultiplier tube array. This secondary electroluminescence signal is commonly referred to as the S2 signal.

Information from the signals in LUX-ZEPLIN#

The S2 signal occurs after the S1 signal because it is delayed by the time it takes the electrons to drift through the xenon. Therefore the time delay between these two signals tells you the depth into the xenon where the event occurred. The direction of the electron drift is directly vertically upwards, and the S2 signal occurs very close to the photomultiplier tube faces. By measuring the ratio of the S2 signals produced in the different photomultipler tubes, an estimate of the two horizontal components of the event can be made. So, using the time delay between S1 and S2 and the horizontal reconstructed position from the sizes of the different photomultiplier signals, a full 3-dimensional reconstruction of the position where the event occurred can be carried out. This will turn out to be crucial for the rejection of background noise in the detector.

Principle of operation of photomultiplier tubes#

A photomultiplier tube consists of a photocathode, a material that emits an electron when light falls on it. Typically the photocathode is a layer of material deposited on the glass or quartz envelope in the input end of the photomultiplier tube. Caesium iodide is commonly used as it has a suitable work function for the liberation of electrons by incoming ultraviolet light.
